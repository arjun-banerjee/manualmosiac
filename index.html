Stitching Photo Mosaics

Background

Given disjoint images of the same scene, we’d like to figure out a way to combine images to develop “stitched” version of the scene that forms a mosaic or panoramic.

In an ideal world, we’d like these photos to be aligned on the same x-y-z rotation axis and completely disjoint, so we can just place the photos next to each other (and maybe apply some blur) to get back the complete scene.

However, capturing full scenes inherently involves rotations (the world is round and we live in 3D) and as such, alignment is not so simple. As such, we make use of homography, where we figure out a linear operation TTT﻿ that maps between common features of the image. We can then solve the system with least-squares, given that it is overdetermined (number of points is at least 4).

Mosaic Generation

To generate mosaics, we do the following steps:

  1. Align the image by warping the second image to the first image
  1. Use Distance Blending. For both the left and right image:
    1. Compute the distance of each pixel from an edge — call this distance matrix DLD_LDL​﻿ and DRD_RDR​﻿
    1. Get the low and high pass components of each image: LR,HR,LL,HLL_R, H_R, L_L, H_LLR​,HR​,LL​,HL​﻿
    1. Create 1 dummy image: defined by B[i][j]=DL[i][j]LL[i][j]+DR[i][j]LR[i][j]B[i][j] = D_L[i][j]L_L[i][j] + D_R[i][j]L_R[i][j]B[i][j]=DL​[i][j]LL​[i][j]+DR​[i][j]LR​[i][j]﻿
    1. Create another dummy image defined by C[i][j]=1(DL[i][j]≥DR[i][j])HL[i][j]+1(DL[i][j]<DR[i][j])HR[i][j]C[i][j] = 1(D_L[i][j] \geq D_R[i][j])H_L[i][j] + 1(D_L[i][j] < D_R[i][j])H_R[i][j]C[i][j]=1(DL​[i][j]≥DR​[i][j])HL​[i][j]+1(DL​[i][j]<DR​[i][j])HR​[i][j]﻿
    1. Sum the images in a 1:3 ratio: Out=14HR+34LROut = \frac{1}{4} H_R + \frac{3}{4} L_ROut=41​HR​+43​LR​﻿
  1. Use Gaussian and Laplacian Stack blending to further isolate edges
    1. Create a mask that defines the region of overlap — then apply a Gaussian filter on the mask and a Laplacian filter on the images. Combine them to yield the final image.
  1. For Additional Aesthetics: CROP THE IMAGE BEFORE GAUSSIAN STACKING

We notice that there are black artifacts bottom and top edges of the above image. I hypothesize that this is because during the masking and blending phases of the Laplacian stack, some of the black pixels of the edges are incorporated into the image when the Gaussian convolution and blurring is applied. To counteract this, I crop the image, and then apply the stacks. Surely enough, this reduces the artifacts! This was the motivation for cropping.
